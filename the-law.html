The law that applies — and where the notices fall short

The notices raise several legal questions. This section sets them out in plain language, with links to the legislation so anyone can check.

1. The wrong Act

The correct law for long-stay residents of a residential park is the Residential Parks (Long-stay Tenants) Act 2006 (WA). Section 8(1) of that Act expressly excludes the Residential Tenancies Act from applying. A notice relying on the wrong Act has no foundation.

Residential Parks (Long-stay Tenants) Act 2006 ↗

2. Who may issue a notice

The Shire holds the licence for the caravan park. Questions arise over whether CMCA had any statutory authority to issue vacation notices in its own name, given the Shire's role as licence holder under the Local Government Act 1995 (ss. 5.42 and 5.44 deal with delegation).

3. The Shire and long-term stays

Under the Caravan Parks and Camping Grounds Act 1995, the licence holder is treated as responsible for the park. The question is whether long-term stays can lawfully be refused in the way the policy attempts.

Caravan Parks and Camping Grounds Act 1995 ↗

4. Notice periods

The notices gave roughly 21 days. The Residential Parks (Long-stay Tenants) Act sets substantially longer minimum notice periods for ending a long-stay arrangement. No-grounds terminations are also tightly restricted under that Act.

5. The 2022 agreement

At the Ordinary Council Meeting of 28 April 2022 (Item 10.4.1, Resolution 22/4/40), it was recorded that long-term tenants "will remain the responsibility of the Shire." That position sits awkwardly with notices issued in CMCA's name in 2026.
