May 2026 — the first report after the notices

For May 2026 the park booked 496 site nights for gross income of $14,552, of which the Shire's 73% share was $10,623 — up 50% on May 2025. Bookings were down on April (589 nights) but year-on-year nights still rose 47% (up 158 nights), and CMCA member stays rose 55%. The site-type breakdown was: powered sites 245, ensuite 130, self-contained RVs 78, unpowered river 40, unpowered van 3 (496 total).

Notably, this report — the first monthly report after the Short Term Stay Policy and vacation notices were introduced — contains no caretaker reference to longer-term stays, essential workers or the housing shortage, despite such references appearing in every report from August 2025 to April 2026. It is framed solely around short-term tourism. Source: Agenda, Ordinary Council Meeting 25 June 2026, Item 10.4.1, pages 24–27.

Key observations

The CMCA caretaker first documented housing-shortage-related demand in August 2025, and did so in every monthly report from then through April 2026 — nine consecutive months before eviction notices were issued.

The January 2026 report marks an escalation: CMCA reported a "significant increase in requests for permanent residency" driven by ongoing housing shortages, and recommended reassessing the maximum stay period — not eviction.

The February 2026 report documented demand from shearing, health, road works, Water Corporation and mineral exploration workers. A two-month limit with case-by-case extension was the policy at that time — not a blanket eviction policy.

The April 2026 report was the last before the Short Term Stay Policy was introduced as Item 10.4.2 at the same meeting. The caretaker's own report did not call for evictions — it called for "actively monitoring" the situation.

Over the eleven months to April 2026 the park generated $122,319 in gross income, of which the Shire received about $89,293; with May 2026 added, the totals reach $136,871 and roughly $99,916. Longer-term stays contributed materially to this revenue, which the Short Term Stay Policy risks reducing.
